Africa DX Antenna Design Report

First Africa Contact from KC7AVA in CN97av

This report evaluates practical antenna choices for making an initial Africa contact from Snohomish County, Washington. The operating goal is a first success on CW or digital, with emphasis on the highest-probability path, realistic installation constraints, and equipment choices suitable for a public-facing station plan.

Primary band: 20 meters Best first-contact mode: FT8 Target heading: ~40° true
Station context: KC7AVA
Grid: CN97av
Objective: First confirmed Africa contact

Executive Summary

Best high-performance option: If the site allows a large fixed wire array, a 20 meter rhombic aimed about 40 degrees true is the strongest directional solution for Africa from CN97av. A 2-lambda design with approximately 140-foot legs offers modeled gain in roughly the 15 to 18 dBi class and also covers much of Europe on the same heading.

Best practical option: A 3-element 20 meter Yagi at roughly 40 to 50 feet, fixed toward 40 degrees or mounted on a rotator, is the most realistic high-value alternative where a rhombic footprint is not available.

Operating strategy: Use 20 meters as the primary band, with 17 meters and 15 meters as secondary bands. For the first success, FT8 on 14.074 MHz offers the highest probability, while CW becomes much more practical once directional gain is added.

Great-Circle Path and Bearing Analysis

From CN97av, the short path to Africa generally runs northeast across the Arctic region, Greenland, and Europe before turning south into the continent. That path favors antennas centered in the northeast quadrant rather than a southeast heading.

Destination region Approximate true bearing from CN97av Notes

Canary Islands / Morocco / Nigeria / South Africa corridor ~50° to 60° Upper end of the Africa arc from the Pacific Northwest.

Geographic center of Africa ~40° A good center heading for a fixed directional antenna.

Aiming at about 40 degrees true is a sound compromise because it centers the full Africa bearing arc and also supports Europe, which often provides a useful indicator of propagation quality on the same path family.

Band and Mode Recommendation

Primary Band

20 meters is the main recommendation. It offers the best balance of path reliability, antenna size, and openings from the Pacific Northwest to Africa.

Secondary Bands

17 meters and 15 meters are worthwhile secondary bands when solar conditions support them, especially on stronger seasonal mornings.

Best First-Contact Mode

FT8 on 14.074 MHz is the easiest first-contact mode. Its low signal threshold materially improves the odds on marginal polar and high-latitude paths.

Option A: 20 Meter Rhombic for Maximum Performance

A properly terminated rhombic is technically strong for this objective. It is a fixed-direction, high-gain wire antenna that trades land area for performance. If the site supports the footprint and support geometry, it is the best dedicated Africa design in this comparison.

Design item Recommended value
Band 20 meters
Heading Center at approximately 40° true
Leg length About 140 feet per leg (2-lambda class design)
Diamond footprint Approximately 118 feet wide by 253 feet long
Recommended site envelope Roughly 130 feet by 275 feet to allow anchor and clearance margin
Gain Approximately 15 to 18 dBi class, modeled and installation-dependent
Termination About 550 to 600 ohms, non-inductive, weatherproofed at the far end
Feed impedance Roughly 600 ohms class

Recommended layout diagram

Feed System

  • Open-wire or ladder line into a balanced tuner is the most traditional approach.
  • A suitable matching transformer or unun can also be used if the system is designed for the expected impedance range and power level.
  • Good common-mode control and weatherproof strain relief remain important.

Mounting and Layout

  • Use four robust supports or a combination of masts and trees with stable end tension.
  • Keep the geometry as symmetrical as the site permits.
  • Preserve the 40° centerline rather than forcing a distorted footprint that shifts the main lobe away from the target arc.

Assessment

  • Best choice for a fixed, high-performance Africa antenna.
  • Particularly strong when FT8 is used during quiet geomagnetic periods.
  • Space requirement is the main limiting factor.

Option B: 3-Element 20 Meter Yagi as the Practical Alternative

A 3-element 20 meter monoband Yagi is the most practical directional alternative. It delivers meaningful forward gain and front-to-back performance without requiring the large land footprint of a rhombic.

Design item Recommended value
Type 3-element 20 meter monoband Yagi
Mounting height About 40 to 50 feet
Aiming Fixed at about 40° true or installed on a rotator
Representative product classes M2, Cushcraft, SteppIR, Spiderbeam, or comparable designs

This option is easier to install, easier to maintain, and more flexible across Africa and Europe because a rotator can adapt to the full 23° to 60° bearing spread. For many stations, it is the best balance between performance and complexity.

Build and Installation Notes

Wire and Hardware

  • Use durable antenna wire sized for local wind and ice conditions.
  • Install quality end insulators, UV-resistant rope, and hardware rated for long-term outdoor service.
  • Where trees are used as supports, allow for movement and seasonal loading.

Termination and Matching

  • For the rhombic, the terminating resistor should be non-inductive, weatherproofed, and mounted to shed water.
  • Keep termination hardware away from casual contact points and combustible surfaces.
  • Match the feed arrangement to actual impedance behavior rather than nominal values alone.

Operating Windows

  • Winter mornings are generally best for the Pacific Northwest to Africa path.
  • Start on 20 meters around 14:00 UTC and continue through about 19:00 UTC as conditions permit.
  • Watch solar and geomagnetic indices; quiet conditions materially improve the high-latitude route.

Safety Notes

Maintain generous power-line clearance at every point of the installation. No support, wire, guy, or feed line should be able to fall into electrical conductors.

Wire ends, feedpoints, and terminating networks can develop substantial RF voltage. Place them beyond normal reach, protect them from weather, and mark service areas clearly before operation or maintenance.

Recommended Path to a First Africa Contact

  1. Prioritize 20 meters for the initial effort.
  2. Use FT8 on 14.074 MHz as the first mode, then move to CW when conditions and station capability allow.
  3. Choose a directional antenna centered near 40° true.
  4. Select the 20 meter rhombic if the property supports a roughly 130 by 275 foot envelope.
  5. Otherwise install a 3-element 20 meter Yagi at roughly 40 to 50 feet as the best practical alternative.
  6. Use 17 meters and 15 meters as secondary opportunities during stronger openings.
